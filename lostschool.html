Lost School

IIA State Award-Gold Leaf-Unbuilt Category 2014

Location   :   Aluva
Year          :   2014

Lost school was about finding the lost spaces left over between buildings and finding images we lost in our childhood. This was done by linking space, time, activity and the memory of shared spaces that forms the essence of any institution.

The project required us to add new class rooms into an existing, functional school, with hardly any available land to build on. The extreme density of the fabric made us look at finding lost spaces between haphazardly constructed existing building and connecting them effectively to form a meaningful whole. Studies were done to understand pre -existing movement patterns. Active currents had to be enhanced, whereas congested currents liberated. This study was then layered over the cleared up/’found’ lost spaces and the new design was formulated... Courtyards with varying sizes & experiential characters (rain court, mango tree court etc) organise the built around them. The project was about finding lost spaces, invigorating existing currents of flow and not losing the connect with memory and place.

The St. Francis High School for Girls, Aluva, is a 100 year old government aided institution, located in the heart of the city. The built fabric of the school, with about 1000 students, was extremely dense, with some of the buildings beyond its time, posing acute safety concerns.

Concerns & Deficiencies
1. Lack of open space for play/ assembly
2. Isolation of building blocks- physical & visual
3. Lack of adequate toilet facilities
4. Outdated buildings- safety concerns
5. Lack of a defined access

Shifting axis
The mango tree becomes the focus that defines the primary axis on entry. A secondary axis is established along the corridor of the new block -which is also an extension of the existing path. The shifting axis is made more animated with the help of the bridging library block that creates a movement cross over.


TOILET

1. The service core is conceived as a physical as well as functional divide between the old and new blocks. It is accessible from all blocks.

2.The toilet block near the entrance, was so located so that the students from the LP school could also access it.(there exists a toilet block near the school- which is to be demolished as per our scheme- so the placement reinstates an existing movement network also).

Recess time, going to toilet etc generally creates a time for children to interact... So courtyards near the toilet block create pockets to contain them for a while

COURTYARDS

The design scheme provides for a large open space which is articulated by several sub-spaces characterized by function, landscape, levels and sensory attributes.

1. Mango tree court. The mango tree, earlier a part of the lost space, defines a outdoor learning area around it, thereby negotiating the existing level differences on site This was done by deleting the toilet block that divided the two spaces.It serves to connect the lower court-(higher secondary block) and the upper larger assembly courtyard.

2. The lower court was kept as it is, so as to maintain the familiar sense of enclosure.

3. Assembly court - With the library block on stilts, one continuous space is created, which can be used for assembly or games.

4. Rain Court Reminiscing about our own school days. Rains were the time we had most fun. Celebrating the monsoons with paper boats, splashing water and hunting for tadpoles... The rain court was thus meant to be a place to connect to the child’s innate emotion of play- also evoking a sense of place and time
